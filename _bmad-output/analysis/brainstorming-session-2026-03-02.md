# Brainstorming Session Results

**Facilitator:** Ducdo
**Date:** 2026-03-02
**Product Name:** RackUp

## Session Overview

**Original Topic:** AI-powered billiards shot assistant mobile app using phone camera for aiming, shot simulation, and table run strategy
**Pivoted To:** RackUp - a Kahoot-meets-Mario-Kart party platform that turns bar games (starting with pool) into chaotic, social, memorable experiences
**Goals:** Explore competitive landscape, validate usefulness, brainstorm features, stay open to alternative ideas

### Context Guidance

_User is an active pool player who struggles with aiming despite using the ghost ball system. Initial concept involved live camera overlay for shot guidance, power calculation, cue ball strike point visualization, and snapshot-based table run planning. Through brainstorming, the concept pivoted away from training/aiming toward social party gaming._

### Session Setup

_Session initialized with AI-Recommended technique approach. Three techniques selected: Question Storming to map the problem space, Analogical Thinking to draw from successful party apps, and SCAMPER to systematically expand the concept._

## Technique Execution Results

### Question Storming (Deep)

**Interactive Focus:** Explored who the target user really is, when/where they'd use the app, what the actual problems are, whether pool even needs an app, and the social dynamics of using tech during a casual game.

**Key Breakthroughs:**
- Beginners don't practice, they just play games - the app needs to feel like play, not homework
- Aim and stroke are separate problems - a camera can't fix stroke
- The social reaction to the app might BE the feature - fun trumps utility
- "Not sure if pool needs an app" was the most honest and productive admission
- Camera phone is most accessible but maybe the camera isn't needed at all

**Creative Pivot:** User responded more strongly to party/social ideas than training ideas, revealing that the real opportunity is entertainment, not education.

### Analogical Thinking (Creative)

**Building on Previous:** Used the social/party direction from Question Storming to analyze successful party platforms.

**Analogues Explored:**
- **Jackbox Games:** Zero friction joining, audience participation, humor is the product, short rounds
- **Kahoot:** Competitive loop (action → result → leaderboard), streak bonuses, music/countdown urgency, personal-then-shared rhythm
- **Mario Kart:** Rubber banding, item system, last place gets best items, blue shell mechanic
- **Among Us:** Hidden roles, deception, suspicion votes, social deduction

**Key Insight:** User strongly preferred the Kahoot model - the shot → result → leaderboard update → next player loop became the core game loop. Mario Kart items became the strategic layer. Among Us deception became an entire game mode.

### SCAMPER Method (Structured)

**Systematic Expansion Results:**

- **Substitute:** Remote pool night, highlight reel auto-generation, AI voice announcer
- **Combine:** Dynamic soundtrack, item trading/negotiation, live spectator mode, pool + storytelling
- **Adapt:** Legacy group progression, player XP system, seasonal leaderboards, confessional reactions, shrinking table mechanic, Duolingo retention nudges
- **Modify:** Quick round mode, team mode, AI voice announcer
- **Put to Other Uses:** Bar partnerships, tournament brackets, corporate team building, first date tool
- **Eliminate:** Phone dark during shots, adaptive single mode that auto-adjusts
- **Reverse:** Loser's League (everyone tries to miss), hidden scores, success punishment

### Creative Facilitation Narrative

_This session featured one of the most dramatic creative pivots possible. Ducdo arrived wanting to build an AI-powered camera aiming assistant for pool - a technically complex, niche training tool. Through collaborative exploration, the real insight emerged: the problem isn't that pool is hard to learn, it's that casual pool nights could be way more fun. The pivot from "billiard bot" to "RackUp" happened organically when party game mechanics (punishments, challenges, social chaos) generated far more enthusiasm than aiming overlays. The camera was dropped entirely. The product became simpler to build AND bigger in market potential._

**Breakthrough Moments:** Party game direction over training tool; Mario Kart rubber banding for inclusivity; Among Us saboteur mode; "missing is fun, not failure" design philosophy
**Energy Flow:** High and consistent throughout. User engaged most deeply with game mode design and social mechanics.

## Session Summary and Insights

### Key Achievements
- **53 ideas generated** across 9 organized themes
- **Major creative pivot** from niche training tool to broad party platform
- **Clear MVP defined** with 4-phase rollout roadmap
- **Unique market position identified:** "The Jackbox of real-world bar games"
- **Core design philosophy established:** "Missing is fun, not failure"

### Breakthrough Insights
1. **The market for "make pool more fun" is 100x bigger than "help me aim."** Training apps serve serious players. Party apps serve everyone who's ever walked up to a bar table.
2. **No camera needed.** Dropping the original camera/CV concept made the product simpler to build, easier to use, and more focused on what actually matters: social fun.
3. **Bad players should have the best time.** The rubber banding, consolation items, and punishment system ensure that the worst player at the table has the funniest, most memorable night.
4. **The app is a social organizer, not just a game.** Seasonal leaderboards, Duolingo nudges, and group progression turn RackUp into the reason friend groups get together regularly.

### Session Reflections
_This session demonstrated the power of structured brainstorming to challenge initial assumptions. The user's honest admission that "I'm not sure if pool needs an app" was the turning point that unlocked the real product. By exploring the problem space through questions rather than jumping to solutions, then drawing from proven party game models (Kahoot, Mario Kart, Among Us, Jackbox), a genuinely novel product concept emerged that neither facilitator nor user had anticipated at the start._
