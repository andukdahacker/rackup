# Product Brief: RackUp

## Executive Summary

RackUp turns bar games into absolute chaos — starting with pool. It's a mobile party app where everyone joins a room on their phones, plays real pool, and the app layers on secret missions, Mario Kart-style power-ups, escalating punishments, and dramatic leaderboard moments. The worse you play, the more fun you have. Every miss drops items, triggers challenges, and fuels chaos. RackUp turns a forgettable Tuesday night into a story your friends retell for weeks.

No app exists that adds party mechanics to real-world bar games — not for pool, not for darts, not for anything. Pool apps today are training tools for serious players. Drinking game apps are generic prompt generators disconnected from any actual activity. RackUp is the first product that bridges real bar games and digital party gaming. "The Jackbox of real-world bar games."

---

## Core Vision

### Problem Statement

Most people at a pool table are bored most of the time. Pool is turn-based with 2-4 players — if you're not shooting, you're standing around holding a beer, watching someone else line up a shot. That's 70-80% of your "pool night" doing nothing. For less skilled players it's worse: they miss, they feel bad, they disengage. By the third game, someone suggests leaving. The night dies — not with a bang, but with a shrug.

There is no product that makes bar games fun for everyone at the table regardless of skill level, eliminates the downtime between turns, and transforms those nights into shareable, memorable experiences.

### Problem Impact

- Casual players — the vast majority — have a mediocre time and don't suggest pool next time
- Friend groups default to passive activities (just drinking) instead of active, structured fun
- Bar nights become forgettable rather than the highlight of the week
- The massive competitive socializing trend (93% Gen Z participation) goes completely unserved in the bar game space

### Why Existing Solutions Fall Short

When you download a pool app today, you get a stats tracker built for league play. When you download a drinking game app, you get generic dares that have nothing to do with the game you're actually playing. Neither makes your Tuesday night at the bar more fun.

- **Pool/billiards apps** (DigitalPool, BilliardManager) — scoring and league management for competitive players. Zero party mechanics.
- **Virtual pool games** (8 Ball Pool, 800M+ downloads) — digital simulations that replace the real game instead of enhancing it.
- **Drinking game apps** (Picolo, PartyPal) — generic prompts disconnected from any physical activity. Shallow, repetitive, no replayability.
- **Competitive socializing venues** (Flight Club, Topgolf) — prove the model works (£80.4M revenue) but require multi-million-pound venue buildouts. RackUp needs just phones and a pool table that already exists.
- **DIY house rules and card games** — the closest thing people have today, but limited, inconsistent, and impossible to sustain across a full night.

### Proposed Solution

RackUp is a mobile-first party platform that layers structured, escalating chaos onto real bar games. One player creates a room, others join via code on their phones. As players take their real shots, the app orchestrates the entire experience:

- **No spectators — everyone is always playing.** The referee role, item system, heckling, and reactions mean there is zero downtime for anyone at the table.
- **Secret missions** give each shooter a private objective for bonus points — only they know what they're attempting.
- **Items and power-ups** (blue shells, immunity, score steals) add a Mario Kart-style strategic layer where last place gets the best drops.
- **Punishments escalate through the night** — mild early, nuclear by last call — creating a deliberate emotional arc from warm-up to peak chaos to epic finale. This isn't random chaos; it's choreographed chaos with a story arc.
- **A dramatic leaderboard** with animations, streaks, and multipliers keeps everyone invested.
- **Multiple game modes** (Party, Total Chaos, Saboteur, Loser's League, Team, Quick Round) ensure every night plays differently.

Core design philosophy: **missing is fun, not failure.** Bad players have the best time. Every miss is a chance for items, punishments, and chaos.

### Key Differentiators

1. **Blue ocean — zero competition.** No app overlays party mechanics onto real-world bar games. Not for pool, not for darts, not for any bar sport. RackUp creates an entirely new product category.
2. **Skill-equalizing design.** Rubber banding, consolation items, and escalating punishments ensure the worst player has the most memorable night. This is the emotional hook — the opposite of every competitive game ever built.
3. **Physical-digital bridge.** RackUp is the only product connecting the digital party gaming market to real-world bar game culture. This is the core innovation and the hardest thing for any competitor to replicate — it requires being physically together, which is both the constraint and the moat.

**Future vision:** Pool is the launch sport. The platform engine is designed to expand to darts, beer pong, cornhole, and other bar sports — each unlocking new geographic markets and demographics.

## Target Users

### Primary Users

**The Friend Group at the Bar (Ages 21-34)**

RackUp is built for groups of friends who are already playing pool — or would play pool if it were more fun. These aren't league players or billiards enthusiasts. They're casual players who picked up a cue because it was there, and they'd rather be laughing than lining up the perfect shot.

**Persona: Jake, 27 — The Social Organizer (Acquisition Target)**
Jake is the one who texts the group chat every Thursday: "Pool tonight?" He's not the best player, but he doesn't care — he's there for the crew. He's always looking for ways to make the night more memorable. He's downloaded every party game app, tried house rules, even brought a deck of cards to the bar once. Nothing sticks. When he discovers RackUp, he's the one who creates the room, explains the rules, and evangelizes it to every friend group he has. Jake is RackUp's champion — the person who downloads the app first and gets everyone else hooked.

*Job to be done:* Organize a memorable night out that everyone talks about afterward.
*What Jake wants:* A reason to get the group together every week. Something that makes the night feel like an event, not just "we went to a bar."
*His aha moment:* The first time the leaderboard flips and the whole group erupts.

**Jake is the acquisition target.** The app's growth funnel is: Jake discovers → Jake downloads → Jake creates a room → everyone else joins via code. Onboarding is designed for Jake's job first; everyone else benefits automatically.

**Persona: Maya, 24 — The Content Creator**
Maya is decent at pool but she's really here for the moments. She's the one filming when someone gets a spicy punishment, screenshotting the post-game report card, and posting the highlight to her Instagram story. For Maya, RackUp isn't just a game — it's content. The shareable report cards, the dramatic leaderboard reveals, the punishment reactions — they're all designed to be captured and posted.

*Job to be done:* Capture and share fun moments that make friends who weren't there jealous enough to come next time.
*Her aha moment:* The post-game report card that roasts the worst player — she shares it immediately.

**Maya is the viral loop.** Jake acquires the group, Maya spreads it beyond the group.

**Persona: Danny, 29 — The Casual Competitor**
Danny loves winning but can't run a table to save his life. In a normal pool game, he's out after three turns and standing around with nothing to do. With RackUp, he's collecting items, executing secret missions, deploying blue shells against first place, and somehow winning despite missing every shot. For Danny, RackUp makes skill irrelevant and chaos king — and that's exactly how he likes it.

*Job to be done:* Have fun and feel competitive even when he's the worst player at the table.
*His aha moment:* He misses his shot, draws a blue shell, nukes first place, and the group loses it.

**Danny is the proof that skill-equalizing design works.** If Danny's having the best time, RackUp is doing its job.

**Persona: Lena, 26 — The Reluctant Player (The Litmus Test)**
Lena got dragged here. She doesn't really play pool and she's already thinking about checking Instagram. But the group needs a referee for the first round, and Jake hands her the phone. Suddenly she's announcing turns like a wrestling commentator, delivering punishment verdicts, voting on who the saboteur is, and controlling the chaos. She never touches a cue. By round three, she's trash-talking louder than anyone. By the end of the night, she's the one who says "when are we doing this again?"

*Job to be done:* Not be bored. Feel included without needing to be good at pool.
*Her aha moment:* She realizes she's been laughing for an hour straight and hasn't checked her phone once.

**Lena is the litmus test.** If RackUp can convert a reluctant non-player into the loudest person at the table, the product works for everyone.

### The Referee (Rotating Role)

The referee isn't a separate user type — it's a rotating role that every player takes turns filling. The referee announces turns, confirms shot results, and delivers missions and punishments with dramatic flair. The role is designed to be fun, not a chore — think wrestling announcer, not scorekeeper. Rotating keeps everyone engaged and ensures nobody is stuck managing the game all night. The referee role is also the key mechanism that eliminates spectators — even when you're not shooting, you have an active role.

### Secondary Users

**Bar Owners & Venue Managers (Future B2B Opportunity)**

Bar owners benefit from RackUp indirectly: the app drives groups to their venue weekly, extends visit duration (one more game turns into three), and creates a reason for repeat visits. The B2B value exchange: RackUp drives recurring foot traffic to your bar. In return, future features could include featured venue placement, event hosting tools (RackUp tournament nights), and customer engagement analytics. This is a Phase 3+ revenue stream, not an MVP concern — but it's worth flagging because it represents a scalable business model beyond consumer subscriptions.

**Expansion Demographic: 30-40 Couples & Groups**

Beyond the core 21-34 demographic, couples and mixed-age groups at bars represent a natural expansion audience. A couple on a date night playing Saboteur mode against another couple, or a 30-something birthday party using Team Mode — these are high-value use cases that don't require any product changes, just broader marketing as the user base grows.

### User Journey

**Discovery** *(Curiosity)* — Jake sees a friend's Instagram story with a RackUp post-game report card. Someone got roasted as "Participant Trophy" winner. It looks hilarious. He downloads the app.

**First Night** *(Surprise & Delight)* — Jake creates a room at the bar, shares the code. His friends join on their phones with minimal friction. They play one round of Party Mode. Within 10 minutes, someone gets their first spicy punishment and the table is in chaos. Lena took the referee role skeptically — she's now announcing shots like a WWE commentator. Everyone is hooked.

**The Loop** *(Social Validation)* — After the game, the post-game report card drops: MVP, worst shot, longest streak, most punishments. Maya screenshots it and posts to the group chat and her Instagram story. Danny is already asking "when's next week?" Lena texts the group: "I can't believe I had that much fun not playing pool."

**Return** *(Anticipation)* — The group comes back next Thursday. They try Saboteur mode. New game, new chaos, new stories. RackUp becomes the reason the group has a standing weekly bar night.

**Expansion** *(Pride)* — Jake introduces RackUp to his other friend groups. Maya's Instagram story brings in curious friends who weren't there. Each new champion repeats the cycle. The app spreads one Jake at a time, one friend group at a time.

### Key Decision Point: Guest Join Model

How guests join a room is a critical product and technical decision that impacts adoption velocity vs. retention:
- **Web join (Jackbox model):** Guests use a mobile web page via room code. Lowest friction, but limited features and no push notifications for re-engagement.
- **App-required (Among Us model):** Everyone downloads. Higher friction to start, but full feature set and retention via push notifications.
- **Hybrid:** Full app for the host/champion, lightweight web experience for guests with a nudge to download for enhanced features (personal stats, items, progression).

This decision should be deferred to technical architecture — the product brief flags it as open.

## Success Metrics

### The Core Question

RackUp succeeds when friend groups make it part of their weekly routine and share the experience beyond the group. Four questions define success:

1. **Do people finish a full game?** (Engagement)
2. **Do they play a second game the same night?** (Stickiness)
3. **Do they bring the app back next week?** (Retention)
4. **Do they invite someone new?** (Growth)

### User Success Metrics

**During the Game:**
- **Game completion rate** — % of rooms that finish a full game (vs. abandoned mid-game). Target: >80%
- **Active participation** — % of players in a room who interact (referee actions, item use, reactions) beyond just shooting. Target: >90%
- **Non-shooter engagement (The Lena Metric)** — % of players who never take a shot but remain active in the room for the full game through referee actions, reactions, and voting. Proves the product works for people who don't even play pool.

**After the Game:**
- **Share rate** — % of games where the in-app share button is tapped on the post-game report card. Target: >40% (success), 70%+ (north star aspiration). Note: this measures share button taps — actual sharing (screenshots, screen recordings, group chat forwards) will be higher but isn't directly measurable.
- **7-Day Group Return** — % of groups that create a new room within 7 days of their last session. This is the single most important user success metric.

### Business Objectives

**3-Month Goals (Validation):**
- Downloads and organic discovery are the primary signal — proof that word-of-mouth is working
- Target: Consistent week-over-week download growth driven by organic sharing, not paid acquisition
- Active groups (rooms created per week) growing alongside downloads — downloads without rooms means onboarding is broken

**12-Month Goals (Product-Market Fit):**
- Weekly active groups as the core business metric — recurring group sessions prove the product is a habit, not a novelty
- Viral coefficient >1 — each active group generates at least one new group through sharing and word-of-mouth
- Retention curve flattens — groups that play 3+ times continue playing indefinitely (the "weekly bar night" behavior)
- Monetization signal — early data on willingness to pay for premium features (theme packs, cosmetics, additional game modes)

### Key Performance Indicators

**MVP KPIs (Instrument from Day One):**

| KPI | What It Measures | Why It Matters |
|-----|-----------------|----------------|
| **Game Completion Rate** | % of started games that finish | Basic health check — is the experience compelling enough to finish? |
| **7-Day Group Return** | % of groups that play again within 7 days | The make-or-break metric for product-market fit |
| **Share Rate** | % of games where share button is tapped | Viral loop — if nobody shares, the product doesn't spread |

**Phase 2 KPIs (Add After Initial Validation):**

| KPI | What It Measures | Why It Matters |
|-----|-----------------|----------------|
| **Viral Replication** | % of guest players who later create their own room as host | The flywheel — guests becoming champions proves the product replicates itself |
| **Weekly Active Groups** | Unique groups creating rooms per week | Core health metric — are groups making RackUp a habit? |
| **Non-Shooter Engagement** | % of non-playing participants active for the full game | The Lena proof point — product works beyond pool players |
| **Group Size** | Average players per room | Bigger groups = more fun = more viral potential |
| **Monetization Signal** | Engagement rate with premium features when offered | Validates willingness to pay |

### The Nightmare Metric

**Downloads without rooms** = the product is failing. If people download RackUp but never create a room or join a game, the onboarding is broken or the value proposition isn't translating. Similarly, **rooms without return** — groups that play once but never come back — signals the first-night experience isn't compelling enough to build a habit.

### Technical Requirement: Group Identity

Measuring 7-Day Group Return requires the ability to identify recurring groups across sessions. Since each room has a new code, group identity must be derived from player composition — e.g., if 3 of 4 players match a previous room, it's the same group. This is a data model requirement that must be solved in MVP, not deferred.

### Strategic Alignment

- **User success drives business success:** Happy groups share → shares drive downloads → downloads create new groups → new groups become recurring → recurring groups prove product-market fit
- **Avoid vanity metrics:** Total downloads without retention data is meaningless. 100 groups playing weekly is worth more than 10,000 downloads with no return visits.
- **Event-driven, not daily:** RackUp is not a daily-use app. Success is measured in weekly group sessions, not DAU. A group that plays every Thursday for 6 months is a perfect user — even if they never open the app on other days.

### Key Decision Points

**Business Model:** The revenue model is not yet decided. Options include freemium with premium game modes, theme pack purchases, cosmetic items, group subscriptions, or B2B venue partnerships. The metrics framework includes a monetization signal KPI to gather early data before committing.

**Monetization Constraint: Never split the party.** Whatever the revenue model, it must not fragment the group experience. If one player pays and others don't, everyone must still access the same game. Either one person pays for the whole group (Jackbox model) or premium features are cosmetic/additive only — never access-gating core game modes during a live session.

## MVP Scope

### Core Features (Launch)

**Room & Multiplayer:**
- Room creation with shareable join code
- 2-8 players per room
- Referee role with rotation — every player takes turns running the show

**Party Mode (One Mode, Done Brilliantly):**
- Real pool game with chaos layer on top
- Turn management with referee confirmation of shot results
- Scoring system with base points, streak bonuses, and final round multiplier (3x)

**Punishments:**
- Pre-built punishment deck with escalation tiers (mild → medium → spicy)
- Custom punishment pool — every player submits one before the game starts
- Automatic escalation as the game progresses

**Secret Missions:**
- Random mission assignment per turn (probability trigger)
- Private mission display — only the shooter sees it
- Bonus points for mission completion
- Full mission deck (bank shots, off-hand, speed demon, trick shots, etc.)

**Items & Power-Ups (Mario Kart System):**
- Item drops on misses (consolation items)
- Rubber banding — last place draws better items
- Item inventory (max 2 per player)
- Launch item deck: Blue Shell, Shield, Score Steal, Streak Breaker, Double Up, Trap Card, Reverse, Immunity, Mulligan, Wildcard

**Leaderboard & Streaks:**
- Dramatic leaderboard with position-shuffle animations after every shot
- Streak fire system (2 = warming up, 3 = on fire, 4+ = unstoppable)
- Final 3 rounds triple points announcement

**Post-Game:**
- Report card with awards: MVP, Sharpshooter, Punching Bag, Participant Trophy, Hot Hand, Mission Master
- Podium ceremony with dramatic 3rd → 2nd → 1st reveal

**Sharing & Viral Engine:**
- Post-game report card — styled shareable image with stats, awards, and roasts. One-tap share to Instagram Stories, TikTok, group chats. This is the MVP viral mechanism.
- "RECORD THIS" prompts — on-screen alerts when shareable moments happen (spicy punishments, blue shells, streak breaks) telling the group to pull out their cameras. Zero engineering cost, authentic user-generated content.
- "Best Moments" timestamps on the report card — "Spicy Punishment at 9:47 PM, Blue Shell at 10:02 PM" — so players can find their own recordings from the night.

**Analytics (MVP KPIs Instrumented):**
- Game completion tracking
- Group identity fingerprinting for 7-Day Group Return measurement
- Share button tap tracking

### Out of Scope for MVP

**Game Modes (Fast Follow — weeks after launch, not months):**
- Saboteur Mode (hidden role + suspicion votes + emergency meetings)
- Total Chaos Mode (app controls everything, random scoring, chaos events)
- Quick Round (5-round blitz, streaks only)
- Team Mode (shared scores, team punishments, item gifting)
- Loser's League (everyone tries to miss, vote on fakers)

**Video Compilation (Phase 2):**
- In-app moment capture — auto-recording of punishment reactions, epic fails, lucky shots
- End-of-night highlight reel — compiled video of the best moments from the full game
- This is the long-term viral vision, deferred until report card sharing validates demand

**Retention & Progression (Phase 2):**
- Player XP and levels (Bar Rookie → Table Regular → Pool Shark)
- Legacy group progression and milestones
- Seasonal leaderboards
- Duolingo-style re-engagement nudges

**Content & Production (Phase 2):**
- AI trash talk commentator / roast engine
- Dynamic soundtrack that reacts to game state
- AI voice announcer
- Evolving nicknames based on play style

**Expansion (Phase 3+):**
- Additional bar sports (darts, beer pong, cornhole, foosball)
- Bar partnership program (B2B venue features, analytics, promotions)
- Tournament bracket system
- Remote pool night (cross-venue play)
- Live spectator mode
- Corporate team building packages
- Theme packs (Horror Night, Date Night, Sports Commentary)

### MVP Success Criteria

The MVP is successful when:

1. **Groups finish games** — Game completion rate >80%
2. **Groups come back** — 7-Day Group Return measurable and trending upward
3. **Groups share** — Share rate >40% on post-game report cards
4. **Organic growth** — Week-over-week download growth without paid acquisition
5. **The Lena test passes** — Non-players actively engage through referee role, reactions, and voting

**Go/No-Go for Phase 2:** If groups are completing games, returning weekly, and sharing content organically after 3 months, proceed to fast-follow game modes and video compilation. If not, diagnose and fix the core loop before adding complexity.

### Future Vision

**Year 1:** RackUp becomes the default app friend groups open when they walk up to a pool table. Party Mode is polished, all 6 game modes are live, and the video highlight compilation turns every night into shareable content. Early monetization through cosmetic items and theme packs.

**Year 2:** Multi-sport expansion — darts unlocks the UK/European market, beer pong and cornhole capture the US college and tailgate demographic. Player progression and seasonal content create long-term retention. Bar partnership pilot launches in key US/UK cities.

**Year 3:** RackUp is "The Jackbox of real-world bar games" — a platform supporting 5+ bar sports across 10+ markets. B2B revenue from venue partnerships, tournament hosting, and corporate team building. The app is the reason friend groups have standing weekly bar nights.
